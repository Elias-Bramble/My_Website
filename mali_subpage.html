The Kingdom of Benin, located in present-day southern Nigeria, thrived from the 13th to the 19th century. Renowned for its sophisticated bronze sculptures and intricate ivory carvings, Benin was a powerful and highly organized state with a complex system of governance led by a divine king, or Oba. Its wealth was largely derived from trade in ivory, spices, and later, slaves. Despite European colonial interference in the late 19th century, Benin's rich cultural heritage endures, influencing contemporary Nigerian society and attracting worldwide interest in its art and history.

Benin's society was structured around a hereditary monarchy, with the Oba at its apex, revered as both a political and spiritual leader. The kingdom's capital,named Edo, was a hub of artistic innovation and cultural exchange, showcasing the kingdom's artistic prowess and economic prosperity to visitors from across Africa and beyond.
